# Clinical Workflow

The Recovery TLV clinical decision system operates on a repeating cycle. Every patient encounter follows the same structure. This is not a protocol — it is a decision architecture.

## The Cycle

```
Listen → Evaluate → Set Goals → Treat → Re-evaluate → Decide
  ↑                                                       |
  └───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The cycle never stops running. Even during treatment, you are listening. Even while listening, you are evaluating. The steps are sequential in logic but overlapping in practice.

---

### 1. Listen

Gather the patient's story without premature interpretation.

- Chief complaint in the patient's own words
- History of present condition (onset, duration, behavior, aggravating/easing factors)
- Previous treatments and their outcomes
- Patient's goals and expectations
- Medical history, medications, imaging
- Psychosocial context (work demands, stress, sleep, beliefs about the condition)
- What the patient thinks is happening (their model of the problem)

**What to listen for beyond the words:**
- Fear-avoidance language ("I'm afraid to move", "they told me my spine is degenerating")
- Catastrophizing ("this will never get better")
- External locus of control ("fix me", "what machine do you use?")
- Incongruence between verbal report and observed behavior
- Yellow flags: beliefs, expectations, and emotional state that may affect recovery

**Output:** A subjective picture of the problem, unfiltered. The patient's narrative is the first data point — not the diagnosis.

---

### 2. Evaluate

Physical examination to generate objective data. The evaluation tests your initial impressions from listening.

**Screening first:**
- Red flag screening (neurological, vascular, systemic, structural) — before anything else
- If red flags detected → REFER. No further evaluation needed.

**Objective examination:**
- Observation (posture, movement patterns, guarding, willingness to move)
- Active and passive range of motion (measured in degrees, documented)
- Strength testing (graded, compared bilaterally)
- Special tests (selected based on hypothesis, not performed as a checklist)
- Neurological screening (dermatomes, myotomes, reflexes — if indicated)
- Functional tests (squat, gait, single leg balance, sit-to-stand, sport-specific)
- Palpation (last, not first — palpation findings are confirmed by movement tests, not the other way around)

**What makes a good evaluation:**
- Every test answers a question. Don't test what you're not going to use.
- Document numbers, not impressions: "45° lumbar flexion" not "reduced flexion"
- Compare bilaterally when possible
- Identify the dominant driver: pain, weakness, stiffness, fear, or instability?

**Formulate the hypothesis:**
Based on listening + evaluation, generate one or two testable hypotheses about what is driving the patient's problem. Not a diagnosis label — a mechanism.

Examples:
- "Segmental hypomobility at L4-L5 is limiting flexion and contributing to local pain"
- "Quadriceps inhibition post-surgery is limiting active extension and stair function"
- "Fear of movement is limiting recovery more than the tissue pathology"

**Output:** Objective baseline data. Measurable. Repeatable. Plus a working hypothesis.

---

### 3. Set Goals

Define what success looks like — before starting treatment. Goals are a contract between you and the patient.

Goals must be:
- **Specific:** "Improve lumbar flexion ROM from 40° to 60°" not "reduce stiffness"
- **Measurable:** Linked to objective tests that can be repeated at re-evaluation
- **Time-bound:** Evaluated within the trial window (3–5 sessions)
- **Functional:** Connected to the patient's real-world needs (not just clinical measures)
- **Agreed upon:** The patient must understand and accept the goals

**Types of goals:**

| Type | Example | Measurement |
|---|---|---|
| ROM | Increase shoulder flexion from 120° to 160° | Goniometry |
| Strength | Restore quadriceps strength to within 10% of contralateral | Dynamometry / rep testing |
| Functional | Walk 30 minutes without pain | Patient report + observation |
| Load tolerance | Return to running 5km | Graded exposure + outcome |
| Independence | Dress independently without assistance | Functional task observation |
| Return to work | Sit at desk for 4 hours without symptoms | Graded exposure |
| Return to sport | Complete training session without modification | Sport-specific testing |

**Set exit criteria at the same time:**
- What improvement do you need to see to justify continuing? (→ CONTINUE criteria)
- What would make you stop? (→ DISCHARGE criteria)
- What would make you refer? (→ REFER criteria)

**Output:** Clear criteria for CONTINUE vs DISCHARGE vs REFER — defined before treatment begins.

---

### 4. Treat

Every intervention is a hypothesis test. You are not "doing physio." You are testing whether your hypothesis about the problem is correct.

**Before treating, answer:**
- What is my hypothesis?
- What intervention tests this hypothesis?
- What response do I expect if the hypothesis is correct?
- What does non-response mean?

**Intervention selection:**
- Select based on the hypothesis, not on the diagnosis label
- One variable at a time when possible (don't change everything at once)
- Apply as a controlled perturbation with a defined dose
- Monitor immediate response (within-session change)

**Dosing principles:**
- Start with the minimum effective dose
- If low dose produces a response → hypothesis is supported, progress the dose
- If increasing dose produces no proportional response → dose-response decoupling → question the hypothesis
- Document: what was done, at what intensity, for how long, and what happened

**Patient education during treatment:**
- Explain what you're doing and why
- Explain what you expect to happen
- Give the patient a role (exercises, behavior modification, self-management)
- Address beliefs and fears directly when they are barriers to recovery

**The intervention answers a question:** "If my hypothesis is correct, this intervention should produce X effect."

**Output:** Data. Did the intervention produce the expected response? If yes → hypothesis supported. If no → hypothesis challenged.

---

### 5. Re-evaluate

Repeat objective measures at defined intervals. Re-evaluation is the most important step because it determines the next decision.

**Re-evaluation protocol:**
- Same tests as initial evaluation (consistency is critical — different tests produce incomparable data)
- Same conditions (time of day, warm-up status, instruction to patient)
- Compare to baseline: is there ≥10% improvement in ROM? Functional progress? Reduced compensation?
- Document the comparison explicitly: "Baseline: 40° flexion. Today: 48°. Change: +20%."

**What to assess at re-evaluation:**

| Signal | Meaning |
|---|---|
| ≥10% improvement in objective measure | Hypothesis supported → CONTINUE |
| Functional milestone achieved | Progress → update goals or DISCHARGE |
| No change in any measure | Non-response → revise hypothesis |
| Same signal for 3+ sessions | Stasis → exit criteria triggered |
| Worsening objective measures | Investigate → possible REFER |
| New symptoms or red flags | → REFER immediately |

**Within-session vs between-session changes:**
- Within-session improvement that doesn't carry over between sessions is not sufficient for CONTINUE
- Between-session improvement (the patient returns measurably better) is the real signal
- If within-session gains always reset to baseline by next visit → the intervention is not producing lasting change

**Patient-reported outcomes:**
- Supplementary, not sufficient alone
- "I feel better" without objective change does not justify continuation
- "I feel worse" with objective improvement may indicate neural sensitivity changes (expected in some conditions) — requires clinical judgment

**Output:** Objective comparison to baseline. Signal or noise? Enough data to decide.

---

### 6. Decide

Apply the decision system. This is not intuition. It is a structured determination based on the data from re-evaluation.

| Condition | Decision |
|---|---|
| Red flags detected at any point | → **REFER** |
| Insufficient data to decide | → **DEFER** |
| Outside physiotherapy scope | → **DECLINE** |
| ≥10% objective improvement within trial | → **CONTINUE** |
| Goals fully met | → **DISCHARGE** |
| No improvement after trial window | → Revise hypothesis or **DISCHARGE** |
| Non-response after hypothesis revision | → **REFER** or **DISCHARGE** |
| Patient choosing to discontinue | → **DISCHARGE** (document) |

**Hypothesis revision (when non-response occurs):**

Before discharging for non-response, ask:
1. Was the hypothesis correct? Could a different mechanism explain the problem?
2. Was the intervention appropriate for the hypothesis? Could a different intervention test the same hypothesis?
3. Was the dose sufficient? Did dose-response coupling exist?
4. Are there confounding factors? (poor sleep, high stress, medication changes, non-compliance)
5. Is there a barrier I missed during listening? (fear, beliefs, secondary gain)

If a revised hypothesis is generated → new TRIAL (3–5 sessions). If revision also fails → DISCHARGE or REFER.

Then the cycle restarts: Listen to the patient again. What has changed? How do they feel? Re-evaluate. Decide.

---

## Decision Points by Session

```
Session 1:    Listen + Evaluate + Set Goals + Formulate Hypothesis
Session 2:    Begin TRIAL → Treat + Monitor Immediate Response
Session 3-5:  Re-evaluate → CONTINUE or Revise Hypothesis or DISCHARGE
Session 6-10: Re-evaluate every 2-3 sessions → CONTINUE or DISCHARGE
Session 10+:  Strong justification required — documented improvement at every checkpoint
```

## The Non-Negotiables

1. **No treatment without a hypothesis.** If you don't know what you're testing, you shouldn't be treating.
2. **No continuation without objective improvement.** Subjective reports alone are insufficient.
3. **No indefinite treatment.** Every case must reach an exit: DISCHARGE, REFER, or DECLINE.
4. **Non-response is data, not failure.** It narrows the hypothesis space and drives the next decision.
5. **Red flags override everything.** No amount of clinical reasoning justifies delaying a referral when red flags are present.
6. **Goals before treatment.** You cannot evaluate success if you haven't defined what success means.
7. **Document everything.** If it's not documented, it didn't happen. If it's not measured, it's not objective.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It's Wrong | What To Do Instead |
|---|---|---|
| Treating without a hypothesis | You can't interpret results without a prediction | Formulate a hypothesis first, then select intervention |
| Repeating the same treatment despite non-response | Repetition without revision is epistemic inertia | Revise hypothesis after 3-5 sessions of non-response |
| Using pain as the only measure | Pain is subjective and unreliable as sole criterion | Measure ROM, strength, function — use pain as supplementary |
| Adding interventions without removing any | Can't determine which variable caused the change | Change one variable at a time when possible |
| Setting vague goals ("get better") | Can't evaluate what you can't measure | Set specific, measurable goals with numbers |
| Continuing because the patient wants to | Patient preference is not a clinical criterion for continuation | If no objective improvement, discuss honestly and DISCHARGE |
| Avoiding DISCHARGE because it feels like failure | DISCHARGE is a valid, positive outcome | Meeting goals = success. Plateau = honest exit. Both are good outcomes. |
| Skipping red flag screening | Red flags missed can cause serious harm | Screen at every session, not just the first |

## When the Patient Doesn't Improve

Non-response is the most informative clinical situation. It tells you more than improvement does.

```
Non-Response
    ↓
Was the hypothesis tested fairly?
    ├── No → Fix the test (dose, technique, compliance)
    └── Yes → Was the hypothesis correct?
              ├── Maybe not → Revise hypothesis → New TRIAL
              └── Probably yes → Is there a confounding factor?
                                ├── Yes → Address it (sleep, stress, fear, beliefs)
                                └── No → DISCHARGE or REFER
```

**Key principle:** If two revised hypotheses both fail to produce improvement, the case is likely beyond the current treatment scope. REFER or DISCHARGE — do not continue searching for a third hypothesis without strong justification.
