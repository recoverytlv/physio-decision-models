# Workplace Ergonomics — Assessment and Recommendations

## Purpose

Many musculoskeletal conditions are maintained or aggravated by workplace factors. Ergonomic assessment and modification are integral to treatment, not optional add-ons.

## When Workplace Ergonomics Matters

| Condition | Workplace Factor |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Neck pain | Monitor height, screen time, phone use |
| Low back pain | Sitting posture, standing desk setup, lifting |
| Shoulder pain | Overhead reaching, mouse/keyboard position |
| Thoracic outlet syndrome | Forward head posture, arm position |
| Carpal tunnel / De Quervain's | Keyboard/mouse ergonomics, repetitive hand use |
| Headache (cervicogenic/tension) | Screen time, lighting, stress |
| Thoracic pain | Prolonged flexion posture |

## Desk/Computer Workstation Assessment

### The Ideal Setup

```
                    ┌──────────────┐
                    │   Monitor    │  ← Top of screen at eye level
                    │              │  ← Arm's length distance (50-70cm)
                    └──────────────┘
                         │
    ┌────────────────────┼────────────────────┐
    │              Desk surface               │  ← Elbow height
    │    [Keyboard]          [Mouse]          │  ← Shoulders relaxed, elbows ~90°
    └─────────────────────────────────────────┘
                         │
                    ┌────┴────┐
                    │  Chair  │  ← Back supported, feet flat on floor
                    │         │  ← Hips at or slightly above knee height
                    └────┬────┘
                         │
                    ┌────┴────┐
                    │  Feet   │  ← Flat on floor or footrest
                    └─────────┘
```

### Checklist

| Element | Correct Setup | Common Error |
|---------|--------------|-------------|
| **Monitor height** | Top of screen at eye level | Too low → neck flexion |
| **Monitor distance** | Arm's length (50-70 cm) | Too close → eye strain, forward head |
| **Monitor position** | Directly in front, not angled | Off to one side → cervical rotation |
| **Dual monitors** | Primary centered, secondary angled | Both off-center → constant rotation |
| **Keyboard** | At elbow height, shoulders relaxed | Too high → shoulder elevation |
| **Mouse** | Close to keyboard, same height | Too far → shoulder abduction |
| **Chair height** | Hips at or slightly above knees | Too low → lumbar flexion |
| **Chair back** | Lumbar support, slight recline (100-110°) | Fully upright (90°) → more disc pressure |
| **Feet** | Flat on floor or footrest | Dangling → thigh pressure |
| **Armrests** | Support forearms, shoulders relaxed | Too high → shoulder elevation |
| **Laptop use** | External keyboard + raised screen | Laptop on lap → everything wrong |

## Standing Desk Guidelines

Standing desks are not automatically better than sitting. The key is **variation**.

### Recommended Protocol
| Time Standing | Time Sitting | Ratio |
|--------------|-------------|-------|
| 20-30 minutes | 20-30 minutes | 1:1 |
| Gradually increase to 45 min standing | 15 min sitting | As tolerated |
| Maximum 2 hours continuous standing | — | Do not exceed without sitting break |

### Standing Desk Setup
- Screen at eye level (may need monitor arm)
- Anti-fatigue mat
- Footrest to alternate foot position
- Comfortable shoes (not heels)
- Don't lean on desk — stand balanced

### Who Should NOT Stand All Day
- Active low back pain aggravated by standing
- Varicose veins or lower limb vascular issues
- Lumbar spinal stenosis (standing worsens symptoms)
- Pregnancy (prolonged standing increases risk)

## Phone/Device Ergonomics

| Issue | Solution |
|-------|---------|
| Cradling phone between ear and shoulder | Headset or speakerphone |
| Looking down at phone | Raise phone to eye level (hold higher) |
| Texting with thumbs for extended periods | Voice-to-text, reduce phone time |
| Tablet on lap | Prop tablet at angle, use stand |

## Movement Breaks — The Most Important Intervention

**The best posture is the NEXT posture.** No position is perfect if held for too long.

### Recommended Break Schedule
| Interval | Activity | Duration |
|----------|---------|---------|
| Every 30 minutes | Micro-break: stand, stretch, change position | 30-60 seconds |
| Every 60 minutes | Short break: walk, movement | 2-5 minutes |
| Every 2 hours | Active break: walk, stairs, longer stretch | 5-10 minutes |

### Quick Desk Exercises (Can Do at Desk)
1. **Chin tucks** — retract chin, hold 5 sec, repeat 10x
2. **Shoulder blade squeezes** — squeeze shoulder blades together, hold 5 sec, repeat 10x
3. **Seated trunk rotation** — rotate left/right, hold 3 sec each side, repeat 5x
4. **Wrist extensions** — extend wrist, hold 15 sec each side
5. **Standing hip flexor stretch** — lunge position, 30 sec each side
6. **Calf raises** — rise on toes, hold 2 sec, repeat 15x

## Lifting Guidelines

### Safe Lifting Principles
1. Plan the lift (where is it going?)
2. Feet shoulder-width apart, stable base
3. Bend at hips AND knees (not just back)
4. Keep load close to body
5. Avoid twisting while lifting — pivot feet instead
6. Don't lift above shoulder height if possible
7. Ask for help with heavy or awkward loads

### Load Limits (General Guidelines)
| Situation | Recommended Limit |
|-----------|------------------|
| Close to body, optimal height | 25 kg (men), 16 kg (women) |
| Extended arms | Reduce by 50% |
| Lifting from floor | Reduce by 30% |
| Repetitive lifting | Reduce by 40-50% |
| Overhead lifting | Reduce by 50-60% |

*These are general guidelines. Individual capacity varies based on fitness, age, and condition.*

## Recommendations by Profession

### Office Workers
- Monitor at eye level, arm's length
- Keyboard at elbow height
- 30-minute movement breaks
- Standing desk option if available
- Consider ergonomic mouse/keyboard if wrist symptoms

### Healthcare Workers (Nurses, Physiotherapists)
- Adjustable treatment tables/beds
- Proper body mechanics during patient transfers
- Use mechanical aids when available
- Rotate between tasks to avoid sustained postures
- Core strengthening as injury prevention

### Manual Laborers
- Proper lifting technique education
- Tool handles at appropriate height
- Job rotation to vary physical demands
- Pre-shift warm-up routine
- Report workplace hazards

### Drivers (Taxi, Truck, Delivery)
- Lumbar support in seat
- Mirror adjustment to minimize twisting
- Regular stops for movement (every 1-2 hours)
- Steering wheel position allowing relaxed shoulders
- Seat position: hips at or slightly above knees
