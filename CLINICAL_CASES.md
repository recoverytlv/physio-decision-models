# Clinical Cases — System in Action

Fictional examples demonstrating how the clinical decision system works in practice. These are not real patient cases.

---

## Case 1: Low Back Pain — Hypothesis Revision After Non-Response

**Intake:** 42-year-old office worker, 3 months of right-sided low back pain radiating to buttock. No red flags. Aggravated by sitting >30 minutes.

**Listen → Evaluate:** Flexion-limited, right-sided paravertebral tenderness, negative neural tension tests, full hip ROM. No neurological deficit.

**Hypothesis:** Segmental dysfunction at L4-L5 contributing to local and referred pain.

**Set Goals:** Reduce sitting intolerance, restore flexion ROM to symmetrical, reduce pain with prolonged sitting.

**TRIAL (Sessions 1–4):**
- Segmental mobilization L4-L5, motor control retraining, graded sitting exposure
- Session 4 re-evaluation: No change in sitting tolerance, no ROM improvement, pain unchanged

**Decision — Non-Response as Signal:**
Hypothesis revised. Segmental mobilization did not produce expected response. Re-examine.

**New Hypothesis:** Hip flexor tightness and anterior pelvic tilt increasing lumbar load during sitting.

**TRIAL (Sessions 5–8):**
- Hip flexor stretching, gluteal strengthening, seated posture modification
- Session 6: Sitting tolerance improved from 30 to 50 minutes
- Session 8: ROM improved 15%, sitting tolerance 60+ minutes

**Decision → CONTINUE** (objective improvement documented)

**Sessions 9–12:** Progressive loading, return to full work capacity.

**Decision → DISCHARGE** (goals met, functional capacity restored)

## Case 2: Shoulder Pain — REFER After Red Flag Detection

**Intake:** 55-year-old woman, progressive right shoulder pain for 6 weeks, no trauma. Pain worsening at night. Reports 4kg weight loss she attributes to stress.

**Listen → Evaluate:** Limited active ROM in all planes. Pain at rest. Night pain. Unexplained weight loss.

**Decision → REFER**

Night pain with unexplained weight loss is a red flag requiring medical investigation before physiotherapy. No TRIAL initiated. Patient referred to physician with documented clinical findings.

## Case 3: Knee Pain — Successful TRIAL to DISCHARGE

**Intake:** 28-year-old runner, anterior knee pain bilateral, worse going downstairs and after running >5km. No locking, no giving way.

**Listen → Evaluate:** Pain with resisted knee extension, positive Clarke's test, weak VMO bilaterally, poor single leg squat biomechanics. No effusion.

**Hypothesis:** Patellofemoral pain syndrome — load exceeds tissue capacity due to quadriceps weakness and poor biomechanics.

**Set Goals:** Run 10km pain-free, descend stairs without pain, restore quadriceps strength symmetry.

**TRIAL (Sessions 1–5):**
- Isometric quad loading → progressive isotonic → single leg exercises
- Session 3: Stair descent pain reduced 50%, single leg squat improved
- Session 5: Running tolerance improved to 8km, quad strength improved 20%

**Decision → CONTINUE** (≥10% improvement in measurable criteria)

**Sessions 6–10:**
- Plyometric loading, running retraining, progressive distance
- Session 10: Running 12km pain-free, stairs pain-free, single leg squat normalized

**Decision → DISCHARGE** (all goals met)

## Case 4: Achilles Tendinopathy — Stasis and Exit

**Intake:** 50-year-old recreational walker, left midportion Achilles pain for 4 months. Morning stiffness 10 minutes. Pain after walking >20 minutes.

**Listen → Evaluate:** Tender midportion Achilles, weak single leg calf raise (8 reps vs 15 contralateral), reduced dorsiflexion.

**Hypothesis:** Midportion tendinopathy with load intolerance.

**Set Goals:** Walk 40 minutes pain-free, restore calf raise symmetry, reduce morning stiffness.

**TRIAL (Sessions 1–5):**
- Isometric calf loading → heavy slow resistance
- Session 3: Morning stiffness reduced to 5 minutes, calf raise improved to 10 reps
- Session 5: Walking tolerance improved to 30 minutes

**Decision → CONTINUE**

**Sessions 6–10:**
- Progressive loading, eccentric emphasis
- Session 8: Calf raise 12 reps (no change from session 6)
- Session 10: Walking tolerance 30 minutes (no change), morning stiffness 5 minutes (no change)

**Decision — Stasis detected:** Three consecutive sessions without measurable improvement. Signal invariance confirmed.

**Options evaluated:**
1. Dose-response decoupling? Increased load intensity at session 7, no proportional response → Yes
2. Hypothesis still valid? Tendon loading is appropriate treatment, but plateau reached
3. Further investigation needed? Consider imaging to evaluate tendon structure

**Decision → REFER** for imaging. If structural changes confirmed, continue modified loading program with realistic expectations. If no structural changes, hypothesis revision needed.

## Case 5: Neck Pain — DECLINE

**Intake:** 35-year-old man, neck pain with bilateral hand numbness, reports difficulty with fine motor tasks (buttoning shirt), feels unsteady walking.

**Listen → Evaluate:** Bilateral upper extremity numbness, hand dexterity deficit, gait disturbance.

**Decision → DECLINE**

Bilateral upper extremity symptoms with hand dexterity deficit and gait disturbance suggest possible cervical myelopathy. This is outside physiotherapy scope for initial management. Patient referred urgently to physician/neurologist. Physiotherapy may be appropriate after medical clearance.

## Case 6: Frozen Shoulder — Respecting the Biology

**Intake:** 52-year-old woman, right-dominant, progressive right shoulder stiffness for 3 months. No trauma. Difficulty reaching behind back, sleeping on right side, and fastening bra.

**Listen → Evaluate:** Capsular pattern restriction: external rotation 20° (contralateral 80°), flexion 110° (contralateral 175°), internal rotation hand to buttock only. Pain at end-range. No red flags. No history of diabetes (risk factor acknowledged).

**Hypothesis:** Adhesive capsulitis, early frozen phase — capsular fibrosis limiting passive ROM.

**Set Goals:** Maintain current ROM (prevent further loss), reduce night pain, improve functional capacity (dressing, reaching).

**TRIAL (Sessions 1–5):**
- Gentle end-range mobilization, ROM exercises within tolerance, sleep position advice
- Session 3: Night pain reduced 40%, ROM unchanged
- Session 5: Night pain minimal, ROM unchanged (external rotation still 20°)

**Decision:** ROM is not improving, but this is expected in the frozen phase. Pain reduction IS an objective improvement. The biology of frozen shoulder means ROM may not change for months.

**Decision → CONTINUE** (pain improvement documented, with explicit acknowledgment that ROM may plateau during frozen phase)

**Sessions 6–12 (over 3 months):**
- Session 8: First ROM change — external rotation 25° (+5°)
- Session 10: External rotation 35°, flexion 130°
- Session 12: External rotation 50°, flexion 150° — thawing phase confirmed

**Decision → CONTINUE** (accelerating ROM gains)

**Sessions 13–16:**
- Progressive strengthening, functional restoration
- Session 16: External rotation 70°, flexion 170°, full functional capacity restored

**Decision → DISCHARGE** (goals met)

**Key lesson:** Frozen shoulder has a biological timeline. The system adapted — pain improvement justified continuation even when ROM was static. Patience is not the same as inertia, as long as some objective signal exists.

## Case 7: Runner's Knee — DEFER Then TRIAL

**Intake:** 33-year-old recreational runner, left knee pain anterior, worse with hills and long runs. Self-diagnosed as "runner's knee." Has been stretching IT band and foam rolling for 6 weeks without improvement.

**Listen → Evaluate:** Anterior knee pain with squatting and stair descent. Pain localized to inferior patellar pole, not peripatellar. Painful with resisted knee extension in 30° flexion. No effusion, no instability.

**Observation:** Pain at the inferior pole of the patella, not around it.

**Decision → DEFER (partial)**

The patient reports "patellofemoral pain" but the examination suggests patellar tendinopathy, not patellofemoral syndrome. These require different interventions. Need to confirm the hypothesis before starting treatment.

**Re-assessment:** Single leg decline squat provokes exact pain at inferior pole. VISA-P score: 52/100. Ultrasound imaging requested.

**Imaging confirms:** Patellar tendinopathy with tendon thickening.

**Revised Hypothesis:** Patellar tendinopathy — load exceeds tendon capacity. Previous treatment (stretching, foam rolling) was inappropriate for tendinopathy.

**TRIAL (Sessions 1–5):**
- Heavy slow resistance protocol, running load management (reduced volume, no hills)
- Session 3: VISA-P score 60 (+8 points), single leg decline pain reduced
- Session 5: VISA-P score 68, running tolerance improved to 5km flat

**Decision → CONTINUE**

**Sessions 6–10:**
- Progressive loading, gradual reintroduction of hills
- Session 10: VISA-P score 82, running 10km with hills, pain minimal

**Decision → DISCHARGE**

**Key lesson:** DEFER is a valid output. The self-diagnosis was wrong, and the previous treatment was based on the wrong hypothesis. Taking time to confirm the hypothesis before treating saved weeks of misdirected treatment.

## Case 8: Post-TKR — Stiffness and Fear

**Intake:** 67-year-old man, 4 weeks post total knee replacement (left). Referred by surgeon. ROM: extension -5° (lacks 5° of full extension), flexion 72°. Walking with frame. Significant quadriceps inhibition.

**Listen:** Patient is afraid to bend the knee further. "The surgeon said I need to get to 90° but it hurts too much." Reports poor sleep due to pain. Wife reports he is "not doing his exercises."

**Listen deeper:** Fear-avoidance. External locus of control ("the surgeon needs to fix this"). Low self-efficacy. Yellow flags identified.

**Hypothesis 1:** Post-surgical stiffness with arthrogenic muscle inhibition.
**Hypothesis 2:** Fear-avoidance is limiting exercise compliance and ROM progression.

**Set Goals:** 0° extension and 90° flexion by 6 weeks post-op. Independent walking with cane. Quad activation sufficient for straight leg raise without lag.

**TRIAL (Sessions 1–5):**
- Pain neuroscience education: "Bending the knee will not damage the implant"
- Graded flexion mobilization with patient controlling the pace
- Quadriceps activation with biofeedback
- Session 3: Extension 0°, flexion 80° (patient doing exercises at home now). Quad lag reduced.
- Session 5: Flexion 92°. Walking with cane. Patient confident.

**Decision → CONTINUE** (ROM milestones met, fear-avoidance reducing)

**Sessions 6–10:**
- Progressive strengthening, stair training, balance
- Session 10: Flexion 115°, full extension, walking independently, stairs reciprocal

**Decision → DISCHARGE** (all goals met, independent functional capacity restored)

**Key lesson:** The barrier was fear, not just stiffness. Addressing the yellow flags (fear-avoidance, low self-efficacy) was as important as the physical treatment. Both hypotheses were valid and addressed simultaneously.

## Case 9: Lateral Hip Pain — What NOT to Do

**Intake:** 48-year-old woman, left lateral hip pain for 6 months. Worse lying on left side, walking, and climbing stairs. Previous physiotherapy: stretching, foam rolling, side-lying hip abduction exercises. Got worse.

**Listen → Evaluate:** Tender over greater trochanter. Pain with single leg stance (30 seconds). Trendelenburg positive. Weak hip abductors bilaterally (left worse). Pain with hip adduction across midline. Pain with side-lying hip abduction.

**Hypothesis:** Gluteal tendinopathy — compressive and tensile overload of the gluteus medius tendon at the greater trochanter.

**Critical observation:** The previous treatment (stretching into adduction, foam rolling the lateral hip, side-lying abduction exercises) was COMPRESSING the tendon further. The previous treatment was making it worse.

**Set Goals:** Reduce night pain (lying on affected side), improve single leg stance tolerance, improve walking endurance.

**TRIAL (Sessions 1–5):**
- STOP: adductor stretching, foam rolling lateral hip, side-lying exercises, crossing legs, standing on one leg
- START: isometric gluteal loading (pain-free), standing abduction with band, gait retraining
- Compression management education: sleep with pillow between knees, don't cross legs, don't hang on one hip
- Session 3: Night pain reduced 60%, single leg stance improved to 20 seconds
- Session 5: Walking tolerance doubled, single leg stance 30+ seconds

**Decision → CONTINUE**

**Sessions 6–10:**
- Progressive isotonic loading, functional training
- Session 10: Walking 40 minutes without pain, stairs pain-free, sleeping on left side

**Decision → DISCHARGE**

**Key lesson:** Sometimes the previous treatment IS the problem. Identifying what NOT to do was more important than what TO do. Gluteal tendinopathy is one of the most commonly mistreated conditions — adductor stretching and foam rolling compress the tendon and make it worse.

## Case 10: Concussion — Multisystem Assessment

**Intake:** 24-year-old male football player, 3 weeks post-concussion. Cleared by neurologist for physiotherapy. Persistent symptoms: headache (daily), dizziness with head movement, neck pain, and exercise intolerance (symptoms worsen after 5 minutes of jogging).

**Listen → Evaluate:**
- Cervical: restricted C1-C2 rotation, deep neck flexor endurance reduced, cervicogenic headache provocation test positive
- Vestibular: positive head impulse test, reduced VOR gain, motion sensitivity quotient elevated
- Exercise tolerance: Buffalo Treadmill Test — symptoms at heart rate 120 bpm (expected >150)

**Three hypotheses identified:**
1. Cervicogenic headache from upper cervical dysfunction (whiplash component)
2. Peripheral vestibular dysfunction (head impulse positive)
3. Autonomic dysregulation (exercise intolerance at low heart rate)

**Set Goals:** Reduce headache frequency, resolve dizziness, complete Buffalo Treadmill Test symptom-free, return to sport.

**TRIAL (Sessions 1–5):**
- C1-C2 mobilization + deep neck flexor training
- VOR exercises (gaze stabilization × 1 and × 2)
- Sub-threshold aerobic exercise (walking at HR 100, progressing)
- Session 3: Headache frequency reduced 50%, dizziness improved with daily activities
- Session 5: Buffalo test symptom-free to HR 140, cervical ROM normalized

**Decision → CONTINUE** (improvement in all three systems)

**Sessions 6–10:**
- Progressive vestibular loading, sport-specific drills
- Graded return to sport protocol initiated
- Session 8: Symptom-free at all exercise intensities
- Session 10: Completed all return-to-sport stages, cleared for full contact practice

**Decision → DISCHARGE** (return to sport protocol completed)

**Key lesson:** Post-concussion syndrome requires systematic assessment of multiple systems. Each system was treated as a separate hypothesis with its own expected response. All three improved, confirming the multi-hypothesis approach.

## How These Cases Demonstrate the System

| Case | Key Decision | System Principle |
|---|---|---|
| Case 1 | Hypothesis revision after non-response | Non-response is diagnostic data |
| Case 2 | Immediate REFER, no TRIAL | Red flags override all other considerations |
| Case 3 | TRIAL → CONTINUE → DISCHARGE | Objective improvement drives continuation |
| Case 4 | CONTINUE → Stasis → REFER | Exit criteria enforce honest termination |
| Case 5 | DECLINE before any treatment | Scope check prevents unsafe treatment |
| Case 6 | CONTINUE despite ROM plateau | Biology must be respected; pain improvement counts |
| Case 7 | DEFER → reassess → correct TRIAL | Wrong hypothesis wastes time; DEFER is valid |
| Case 8 | Address fear-avoidance alongside stiffness | Yellow flags are as important as physical findings |
| Case 9 | Previous treatment was the problem | Knowing what NOT to do matters |
| Case 10 | Multi-hypothesis approach | Complex cases need systematic multi-system assessment |

In every case, the workflow is the same: **Listen → Evaluate → Set Goals → Treat → Re-evaluate → Decide.**

The decision is never "keep treating." It is always one of: DECLINE, DEFER, REFER, TRIAL, CONTINUE, or DISCHARGE — with documented justification.
