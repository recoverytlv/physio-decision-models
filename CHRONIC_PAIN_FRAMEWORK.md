# Chronic Pain Framework — Within the Clinical Decision System

## Definition

Chronic pain: Pain persisting beyond normal tissue healing time (generally >3 months). In chronic pain, the relationship between tissue pathology and pain experience is often decoupled — pain persists after tissues have healed.

## Scope Boundaries

- **IN scope:** Chronic musculoskeletal pain with identifiable contributing factors (deconditioning, fear-avoidance, central sensitization, sleep, ergonomics)
- **OUT of scope:** Chronic pain requiring pharmacological management as primary intervention (REFER to pain medicine), chronic pain with untreated psychiatric comorbidity (REFER to psychology/psychiatry)

## How Chronic Pain Differs from Acute Pain

| Feature | Acute Pain | Chronic Pain |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| Duration | <3 months | >3 months |
| Tissue correlation | Usually proportional | Often decoupled |
| Primary driver | Peripheral (tissue injury) | Central (nervous system sensitization) |
| Treatment focus | Tissue healing, protection | Nervous system calming, function restoration |
| Exercise approach | Protect → load gradually | Graded exposure, pacing, confidence |
| Goal | Full recovery | Functional restoration, self-management |
| Timeline expectations | Weeks | Months |
| Patient education | Injury-specific | Pain neuroscience education |
| Trial window | Standard 3-5 sessions | Extended 6-10 sessions |

## The Chronic Pain Assessment Framework

### 1. Biological Factors
- Tissue pathology (what's left from original injury, if anything)
- Deconditioning level (how much physical capacity has been lost)
- Central sensitization markers (widespread pain, allodynia, hyperalgesia)
- Sleep quality (pain amplifier #1)

### 2. Psychological Factors
- Fear-avoidance beliefs (FABQ) — "I'm afraid to move because I'll make it worse"
- Catastrophizing (PCS) — "This will never get better, something terrible is wrong"
- Depression (PHQ-9) — Commonly co-exists, amplifies pain
- Self-efficacy — "Do I believe I can manage this?"
- Past treatment experiences — "Everything has failed, nothing works"

### 3. Social Factors
- Work status and satisfaction
- Social support vs isolation
- Financial stress from pain/disability
- Medico-legal involvement (compensation, litigation)
- Cultural beliefs about pain and disability

## Clinical Hypothesis Framework for Chronic Pain

### Hypothesis 1: Deconditioning as Primary Driver
- **Presentation:** Pain with activity, fear of movement, reduced physical capacity
- **Test:** Graded exercise program starting well below symptom threshold
- **Expected response:** Improved tolerance to activity within 4-6 weeks
- **MCID:** Functional test improvement (6MWT, sit-to-stand, PSFS)

### Hypothesis 2: Fear-Avoidance as Primary Driver
- **Presentation:** High FABQ, avoidance of specific movements/activities, disproportionate pain behavior
- **Test:** Pain neuroscience education + graded exposure to feared movements
- **Expected response:** Reduced FABQ scores, increased willingness to engage in activities
- **MCID:** FABQ reduction ≥4 points, PSFS improvement ≥2 points

### Hypothesis 3: Central Sensitization as Primary Driver
- **Presentation:** Widespread pain, allodynia, hyperalgesia, pain disproportionate to findings
- **Test:** Low-intensity aerobic exercise + pain neuroscience education + sleep optimization
- **Expected response:** Gradual reduction in pain sensitivity and widespread symptoms
- **MCID:** Pain area reduction, improved exercise tolerance, better sleep

### Hypothesis 4: Sleep Disruption as Primary Driver
- **Presentation:** Poor sleep quality, fatigue, pain worse in morning, daytime dysfunction
- **Test:** Sleep hygiene education, exercise timing optimization, relaxation training
- **Expected response:** Improved sleep quality correlates with pain reduction
- **If sleep disorder suspected:** REFER to sleep medicine

### Hypothesis 5: Psychological Distress as Primary Driver
- **Presentation:** PHQ-9 ≥15, PCS ≥30, significant life stressors, trauma history
- **Test:** Address within physio scope (education, exercise, validation) AND REFER to psychology
- **Expected response:** Multidisciplinary approach needed — physio alone insufficient
- **Decision:** REFER for psychology. Can continue physio concurrently if functional goals active.

## Treatment Principles for Chronic Pain

### 1. Validate First
- "Your pain is real."
- "We're not going to dismiss what you're feeling."
- "Many people with persistent pain improve significantly with the right approach."

### 2. Educate (Pain Neuroscience Education)
- See [Pain Neuroscience Education](PAIN_NEUROSCIENCE_EDUCATION.md) for full patient guide
- Pain ≠ damage (especially after healing timeframe)
- The nervous system can be de-sensitized
- Multiple factors influence pain (sleep, stress, beliefs, activity)

### 3. Grade the Exposure
| Phase | Duration | Approach |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Phase 1 | Weeks 1-3 | Establish baseline. Start BELOW current tolerance. Build trust. |
| Phase 2 | Weeks 4-8 | Gradual increase. 5-10% per week. Introduce feared activities. |
| Phase 3 | Weeks 9-12 | Functional goals. Return to meaningful activities. |
| Phase 4 | Ongoing | Self-management. Independent program. Flare-up plan. |

### 4. Address Contributing Factors
- Sleep: Hygiene, timing, environment
- Stress: Management strategies, breathing, mindfulness referral
- Beliefs: Challenge unhelpful beliefs through education AND experience
- Activity: Pacing (avoid boom-bust cycle)

### 5. Set Functional Goals (Not Pain Goals)
| Instead of... | Set this goal... |
|--------------|-----------------|
| "Reduce my pain from 7 to 3" | "Walk 30 minutes without stopping" |
| "No pain at work" | "Complete a full workday sitting/standing" |
| "Pain-free sleep" | "Sleep 6+ hours without waking" |
| "Eliminate all pain" | "Return to swimming twice a week" |

## Decision Criteria — Adapted for Chronic Pain

### Extended TRIAL (6-10 sessions)
Standard 3-5 session trial is often insufficient for chronic pain. Extended trial is justified when:
- Pain duration >6 months
- Multiple contributing factors identified
- Patient needs time to build trust and understand the approach
- Graded exercise requires longer to show functional gains

### CONTINUE if:
- Functional improvement documented (PSFS, 6MWT, sit-to-stand, work tolerance)
- Reduced fear-avoidance (FABQ improvement)
- Better self-efficacy
- Improved sleep
- Patient engagement with self-management
- **Note:** Pain level may not change initially even with functional improvement — this is expected

### DISCHARGE if:
- Functional goals met
- Independent self-management program
- Flare-up plan understood and practiced
- Patient confident in managing independently

### REFER if:
- Severe psychological distress beyond physiotherapy scope
- Suicidal ideation
- No functional improvement after 10-12 sessions of multimodal approach
- Need for multidisciplinary pain program
- Pharmacological management needed
- Sleep disorder requiring medical intervention

## Common Mistakes in Chronic Pain Management

| Mistake | Why It's Harmful | Better Approach |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Treating chronic pain like acute pain | Passive modalities and rest reinforce disability | Active approach: exercise, education, function |
| Pursuing diagnosis endlessly | Repeated imaging/specialist visits reinforce illness beliefs | Explain that diagnosis doesn't change treatment plan |
| Setting pain reduction as primary goal | Pain may not reduce initially; focusing on it increases attention to pain | Function-focused goals |
| Pushing through pain aggressively | Reinforces threat, causes flares, breaks trust | Graded exposure within manageable levels |
| Dismissing the patient's experience | Breaks therapeutic alliance, reinforces "nobody believes me" | Validate first, then educate |
| Using only passive treatments | Creates dependency, no long-term benefit | Active self-management as primary intervention |
| Treating indefinitely without review | Accumulated decision error, dependency | Regular review against functional goals |

## When to Exit Chronic Pain Management

Even chronic pain cases must have exit criteria:
1. **Functional goals met** → DISCHARGE with self-management plan
2. **Functional plateau despite optimal approach** → DISCHARGE or REFER to pain program
3. **Patient not engaging** → Honest conversation, reassess goals, consider DISCHARGE
4. **Psychological factors dominating** → REFER to psychology, may continue physio concurrently
5. **No functional change after 10-12 sessions** → REFER for multidisciplinary assessment
