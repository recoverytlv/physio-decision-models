# Dose-Response Coupling

## Premise
In a responsive biological system, changes in intervention parameters should produce proportional changes in system output.

## Definition
Dose-response coupling is the measurable relationship between intervention intensity (frequency, duration, load, complexity) and the magnitude or direction of the observed clinical response.

## Clinical Interpretation
- **Coupled response:** Increased dose produces proportional improvement — hypothesis is supported.
- **Decoupled response:** Increased dose produces no change or disproportionate change — hypothesis is weakened.
- **Inverse response:** Increased dose produces worsening — hypothesis is falsified or safety boundary is reached.

## Decision Consequences
- Dose-response decoupling is a signal of hypothesis failure, not insufficient dosage.
- Escalating intensity without proportional response increases iatrogenic risk without diagnostic value.
- Persistent decoupling after parameter adjustment mandates exit or referral.

## Threshold Integration
When dose-response decoupling persists across two or more intervention cycles, exit criteria as defined in `thresholds/exit-criteria-stasis.md` are triggered.
