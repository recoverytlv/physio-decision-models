# Geriatric Considerations in Physiotherapy

## Purpose

Older adults (≥65 years) present unique clinical considerations that affect assessment, goal-setting, treatment dosing, and discharge criteria. This document adapts the clinical decision system for the geriatric population while maintaining the same decision logic.

## Key Differences in Geriatric Physiotherapy

### 1. Goals May Differ
| Younger Adult | Older Adult |
|--------------|-------------|
| Return to sport | Return to independent function |
| Full ROM recovery | Functional ROM (enough for daily tasks) |
| Maximum strength | Strength for safety (transfers, balance, stairs) |
| Pain-free | Pain-manageable, functional |
| Pre-injury level | Optimize within current capacity |

### 2. Timelines Are Different
- Recovery is slower due to reduced tissue healing capacity
- Trial window may need extension (5-8 sessions instead of 3-5)
- Progress should still be measurable — just allow more time between assessments
- The decision logic is the same: no improvement → hypothesis revision → exit

### 3. Comorbidities Affect Everything
Common comorbidities that affect physiotherapy:
- Cardiovascular disease → exercise intensity limits, vital sign monitoring
- Diabetes → wound healing, peripheral neuropathy, hypoglycemia risk with exercise
- Osteoporosis → fracture risk with falls, exercise modification
- Cognitive decline → exercise adherence, safety with home program
- Polypharmacy → dizziness (falls risk), drug interactions affecting exercise response
- Visual/hearing impairment → communication, safety

## Fall Prevention — A Primary Goal

Falls are the leading cause of injury-related hospitalization in older adults. Every geriatric physiotherapy case should include fall risk assessment.

### Fall Risk Screening

| Test | Cut-off | Interpretation |
|------|---------|---------------|
| Timed Up and Go (TUG) | >13.5 seconds | Increased fall risk |
| 30-Second Sit-to-Stand | <8 repetitions | Lower limb weakness |
| Single Leg Stance | <5 seconds | Balance deficit |
| Berg Balance Scale | <45/56 | Fall risk |
| Falls History | ≥2 falls in past year | High risk |
| Fear of Falling (FES-I) | >23 | Significant fear — limits activity |

### Fall Risk Factors to Assess
- [ ] Previous falls
- [ ] Medications (sedatives, antihypertensives, polypharmacy ≥4 medications)
- [ ] Vision impairment
- [ ] Lower limb weakness
- [ ] Balance impairment
- [ ] Gait abnormality
- [ ] Environmental hazards (home assessment if possible)
- [ ] Cognitive impairment
- [ ] Urinary urgency/incontinence
- [ ] Fear of falling

## Exercise Prescription for Older Adults

### Evidence-Based Recommendations

| Component | Recommendation | Frequency | Evidence |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|---------|
| Strength training | Progressive resistance, major muscle groups | 2-3x/week | Strong — reduces fall risk, improves function |
| Balance training | Static → dynamic → reactive, dual-task | 3x/week (≥3 hours total) | Strong — strongest fall prevention evidence |
| Aerobic | Walking, cycling, swimming | 150 min/week moderate | Strong — cardiovascular health, endurance |
| Flexibility | Major joints, hold 30-60 seconds | Daily if possible | Moderate — maintains ROM for function |
| Functional training | Transfers, stairs, reaching, lifting | Integrated into program | Strong — most directly translates to independence |

### Exercise Modification Principles
- **Start lower** than you think necessary — especially post-hospitalization or after prolonged inactivity
- **Progress slower** — 5-10% per week instead of 10-20%
- **Monitor response** — next-day symptoms, fatigue levels, vital signs if indicated
- **Chair-based options** for patients with severe balance or mobility limitations
- **Seated to standing progression** for strength exercises

### Vitale Signs Monitoring During Exercise
Required if cardiac history, uncontrolled hypertension, or new exercise program in sedentary patient:
- Rest before exercise: HR, BP
- Stop exercise if: HR >80% age-predicted max, systolic BP >200, new chest pain, dizziness, undue breathlessness

## Cognitive Considerations

### Mild Cognitive Impairment
- **Exercise still works** — evidence supports exercise for cognitive and physical benefits
- **Simplify programs** — fewer exercises, clear written/visual instructions
- **Caregiver involvement** — train family/caregiver to assist with home program
- **Consistent routine** — same time, same place, same sequence

### Dementia
- **IN scope:** Fall prevention, mobility maintenance, functional exercise with supervision
- **Approach:** Simple, repetitive exercises. Visual demonstration over verbal instruction. Music-assisted movement may improve engagement
- **Caregiver training** is as important as patient treatment
- **REFER if:** Behavioral symptoms interfere with safe assessment or treatment

## Geriatric-Specific Red Flags

| Red Flag | Concern | Action |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Unexplained weight loss >5% in 6 months | Malignancy, depression, malnutrition | REFER to physician |
| New confusion or cognitive decline | Infection, medication side effects, stroke | REFER urgently |
| Fall with head strike | Subdural hematoma (especially on anticoagulants) | REFER to ER |
| Sudden onset unilateral weakness | Stroke/TIA | REFER to ER immediately |
| New incontinence | Cauda equina, neurological condition | REFER |
| Acute hot swollen joint | Septic arthritis, gout, fracture | REFER |
| Progressive bilateral leg weakness | Spinal cord compression | REFER urgently |

## Decision Criteria — Geriatric Adaptation

### TRIAL (Extended: 5-8 sessions)
- Allow more time for adaptation to exercise
- Measure same outcomes but expect smaller magnitudes of change
- Functional measures (TUG, sit-to-stand) are most relevant

### CONTINUE if:
- Functional improvement documented (even small gains are meaningful)
- TUG improvement ≥2.5 seconds
- Sit-to-stand improvement ≥2 repetitions
- Improved confidence / reduced fear of falling
- Better performance with daily tasks (reported AND observed)

### DISCHARGE if:
- Functional goals met (may be "maintenance" level, not full recovery)
- Independent or supervised home program established
- Caregiver trained if applicable
- Fall prevention strategies implemented

### REFER if:
- Red flags detected
- Functional decline despite structured program
- Need for multidisciplinary geriatric assessment
- Cognitive or psychological barriers beyond physiotherapy scope
- Need for home environment modification assessment

## The Dignity Principle

Every older adult deserves:
- To be heard — not rushed, not dismissed
- To have their goals respected — even if "just want to walk to the mailbox"
- To receive evidence-based care — age is not a reason for substandard treatment
- To have honest conversations — about realistic expectations AND about what is possible
- To not be patronized — many older adults are more resilient than assumed
