# Home Exercise Principles — Patient Guide

## Why Home Exercise Matters

Physiotherapy sessions are 30-60 minutes. There are 168 hours in a week. What you do between sessions determines your recovery more than what happens during sessions. Home exercise is not optional — it is the treatment.

## Core Principles

### 1. Consistency Over Intensity
- A moderate exercise done daily beats an intense exercise done once a week
- Aim for frequency first, then increase intensity
- Missing one day is fine. Missing five days loses progress.

### 2. Pain Is Information, Not a Stop Sign
- Mild discomfort during exercise (3-4/10) is acceptable
- Pain that worsens significantly during exercise → stop and tell your physiotherapist
- Pain that increases AFTER exercise and lasts >24 hours → exercise was too intense

### Pain Traffic Light System

| Signal | Pain Level | Action |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Green | 0-3/10, settles quickly | Continue as prescribed |
| Yellow | 4-5/10 during exercise, settles within 2 hours after | Acceptable but monitor. May need modification |
| Red | >5/10 during exercise, OR increases after, OR lasts >24 hours | Too much. Reduce load/reps/range. Tell your physiotherapist |

### 3. Progressive Overload
Your body adapts to the demands placed on it. To continue improving:
- Start with what you can do comfortably
- Increase gradually (10-20% per week)
- Your physiotherapist will guide progression — do not jump ahead

### 4. Quality Over Quantity
- Perform exercises with correct form
- Fewer repetitions done well are better than more done poorly
- If you cannot maintain form → reduce the load or take a rest

### 5. Exercise Is Specific
- Exercises are prescribed for YOUR condition, not generic
- Do not substitute exercises from YouTube or friends
- If an exercise doesn't make sense to you, ask — understanding the "why" improves adherence

## Common Exercise Types and Their Purpose

| Exercise Type | Purpose | Example |
|--------------|---------|---------|
| Stretching | Restore flexibility, reduce stiffness | Hamstring stretch, calf stretch |
| Strengthening | Build muscle capacity to support joints | Quad sets, bridges, cuff exercises |
| Motor control | Retrain movement patterns | Deep neck flexor activation, core stability |
| Aerobic | Cardiovascular fitness, pain modulation | Walking, cycling, swimming |
| Balance | Proprioception, fall prevention | Single leg stance, balance board |
| Functional | Replicate real-life tasks | Stair practice, reaching, lifting |

## How to Track Progress

Your physiotherapist will measure objective outcomes, but you can track:
- Number of repetitions completed
- Weight/resistance used
- Range of motion (how far you can reach/bend)
- Pain level during exercise (0-10)
- Functional milestones (can walk 20 minutes, can climb stairs without pain)

## When to Contact Your Physiotherapist

- Exercise causes sharp, sudden pain
- Symptoms are getting worse despite exercise
- You don't understand how to do an exercise
- You had a fall, re-injury, or setback
- New symptoms appear (numbness, weakness, swelling)

## Flare-Up Management

Flare-ups happen. They do not mean you've "damaged" something. Here's what to do:

1. **Don't panic** — flare-ups are temporary increases in symptoms
2. **Don't stop completely** — reduce to 50% of current level
3. **Gentle movement** — walking, light stretching at comfortable level
4. **Ice or heat** — whichever feels better for you (15-20 minutes)
5. **Gradual return** — increase back to normal over 3-7 days
6. **Identify triggers** — did you do too much? Sleep poorly? High stress?

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How many times a day should I do my exercises?**
A: Follow your physiotherapist's prescription. Generally 1-2 times daily for most conditions.

**Q: Should I exercise if I'm in pain?**
A: Mild pain (green/yellow zone) is acceptable. See the Pain Traffic Light above. If in doubt, ask your physiotherapist.

**Q: Can I do other exercise (gym, running, swimming) alongside my program?**
A: Usually yes, but check with your physiotherapist. Some activities may need modification during recovery.

**Q: I forgot how to do an exercise. What should I do?**
A: Do not guess. Contact your physiotherapist or wait until your next session. Incorrect exercise can be counterproductive.

**Q: My exercises feel too easy. Should I do more?**
A: Tell your physiotherapist at your next session. They'll progress your program. Do not increase on your own.
