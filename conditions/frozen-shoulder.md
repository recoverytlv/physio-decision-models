# Frozen Shoulder (Adhesive Capsulitis)

## Condition
Adhesive capsulitis of the glenohumeral joint. Progressive loss of active and passive range of motion in a capsular pattern (external rotation most limited, followed by abduction and internal rotation). Typically affects ages 40–60.

## Scope Check
Frozen shoulder is within physiotherapy scope. However, the condition has a natural history (freezing, frozen, thawing) that must be acknowledged. Physiotherapy manages symptoms and function but cannot override the biological timeline. Suspected underlying pathology (tumour, infection, fracture) triggers REFER.

## Red Flags (→ REFER)
- Shoulder stiffness following trauma (rule out fracture, dislocation)
- Progressive pain with systemic symptoms (weight loss, night sweats, fever)
- History of malignancy with new shoulder symptoms
- Rapidly worsening stiffness not consistent with adhesive capsulitis timeline
- Failed conservative management (consider manipulation under anesthesia or hydrodilatation)

## Clinical Hypothesis Examples
- **Early freezing phase — pain-dominant:** Capsular inflammation limiting motion through pain. Tested with gentle ROM within tolerance, pain education, activity modification. Expected response: maintained ROM, reduced pain intensity.
- **Frozen phase — stiffness-dominant:** Capsular fibrosis limiting passive ROM. Tested with sustained stretching, joint mobilization at end-range. Expected response: gradual ROM improvement (may be slow — 1-2° per week is meaningful).
- **Thawing phase — recovering motion:** Capsule softening with improving ROM. Tested with progressive stretching and strengthening. Expected response: accelerating ROM gains and functional improvement.
- **Secondary frozen shoulder:** Stiffness following rotator cuff surgery, fracture, or immobilization. Tested with aggressive ROM program (within surgical limits). Expected response: ROM improvement with structured program.

## Trial Window
5 sessions (frozen shoulder typically requires longer observation). Objective criteria:
- Any measurable improvement in passive ROM (even 5° is signal in frozen shoulder)
- Reduced pain at rest or at night
- Improved functional capacity (reaching, dressing, sleeping position)

## Non-Response Considerations
Frozen shoulder has a prolonged natural history (12–30 months). Lack of ROM improvement over 5 sessions does not necessarily indicate hypothesis failure — it may indicate the patient is in the frozen phase. Decision:
- If pain is the primary complaint and pain is reducing → CONTINUE
- If ROM is the primary complaint and no change in 8+ sessions → discuss surgical options (REFER for hydrodilatation or MUA)
