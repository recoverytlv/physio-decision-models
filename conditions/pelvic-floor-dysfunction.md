# Pelvic Floor Dysfunction

## Definition
Pelvic floor dysfunction encompasses hypertonic (overactive) and hypotonic (weak) pelvic floor conditions presenting as pelvic pain, urinary symptoms, or functional limitations. Within physiotherapy scope: musculoskeletal pelvic pain, coccydynia, dyspareunia with muscular origin, and post-surgical pelvic rehabilitation.

## Scope Boundaries
- **IN scope:** Musculoskeletal pelvic pain, coccydynia, post-surgical pelvic rehab (post-prostatectomy, post-hysterectomy), SI joint-related pelvic floor dysfunction
- **OUT of scope:** Primary urological/gynecological pathology, prolapse management (REFER to pelvic floor specialist), obstetric conditions

## Red Flags → REFER or DECLINE
- Unexplained rectal bleeding
- Unexplained weight loss with pelvic symptoms
- New-onset bowel/bladder incontinence with neurological signs (cauda equina)
- Pelvic mass on examination
- Post-menopausal bleeding
- Suspected pelvic fracture

## Clinical Hypothesis Framework

### Hypertonic Pelvic Floor
- **Hypothesis:** Pelvic floor overactivity is driving pain and dysfunction
- **Test:** Diaphragmatic breathing, manual release techniques, relaxation training
- **Expected response:** Pain reduction ≥2/10 VAS, improved sitting tolerance within 3-5 sessions
- **If no response:** Consider visceral referral source, central sensitization, or psychological factors → REFER

### Coccydynia
- **Hypothesis:** Coccyx pain is musculoskeletal (post-fall, postpartum, prolonged sitting)
- **Test:** Manual therapy to coccyx and surrounding structures, sitting modification, pelvic floor relaxation
- **Expected response:** Reduced pain with sitting within 3-5 sessions
- **If no response:** Imaging to rule out fracture/tumor → REFER

### Post-Surgical (Prostatectomy/Hysterectomy)
- **Hypothesis:** Pelvic floor weakness is contributing to functional limitation
- **Test:** Progressive pelvic floor strengthening, motor control retraining
- **Expected response:** Improved continence, reduced leakage episodes within 4-6 weeks
- **If no response:** REFER to pelvic floor specialist for biofeedback or further assessment

## Trial Protocol (TRIAL)
| Session | Focus |
|---------|-------|
| 1 | Assessment: symptom mapping, functional baseline, identify hypertonic vs hypotonic pattern |
| 2 | Intervention: targeted relaxation (hypertonic) or strengthening (hypotonic) |
| 3 | Re-evaluate: symptom change, functional improvement |
| 4-5 | Progress or exit decision |

## Decision Criteria

### CONTINUE if:
- Pain reduction ≥2/10 VAS
- Improved functional tolerance (sitting, standing, walking)
- Measurable strength or relaxation gains

### DISCHARGE if:
- Functional goals met
- Independent home program established

### REFER if:
- Red flags detected
- No response after 5 sessions
- Symptoms suggest primary urological/gynecological pathology
- Complex prolapse or severe incontinence

## Common Mistake
Treating all pelvic floor dysfunction with Kegels. Hypertonic pelvic floors need relaxation, not strengthening. Assessment determines direction.
