# Total Hip Replacement Rehabilitation

## Condition
Post-operative rehabilitation following total hip arthroplasty (THA). Includes anterior, posterior, and lateral surgical approaches, each with specific precautions.

## Scope Check
Post-surgical hip replacement rehabilitation is within physiotherapy scope when cleared by the surgeon. Signs of surgical complication (dislocation, infection, DVT, fracture) require immediate REFER.

## Red Flags (→ REFER)
- Sudden severe hip pain with leg shortening and rotation (suspected dislocation — emergency)
- Calf pain, swelling, and redness (suspected DVT)
- Sudden shortness of breath (suspected pulmonary embolism — emergency)
- Wound infection signs (increasing redness, warmth, drainage, fever)
- New-onset thigh pain with weight bearing (periprosthetic fracture)
- Progressive leg length discrepancy or new limp not improving with rehabilitation

## Approach-Specific Precautions

| Approach | Precautions | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| **Posterior** | No hip flexion >90°, no internal rotation, no adduction past midline | 6–12 weeks |
| **Anterior** | No hip extension past neutral, no external rotation with extension | 2–6 weeks |
| **Lateral** | Protect hip abductors, limit active abduction initially | 6 weeks |

## Clinical Hypothesis Examples
- **Post-surgical deconditioning:** Weakness and reduced endurance from pre-operative disability and surgical recovery. Tested with progressive strengthening and cardiovascular conditioning. Expected response: improved walking distance, stair ability, and independence within each phase.
- **Hip abductor weakness:** Gluteus medius weakness causing Trendelenburg gait. Tested with progressive hip abductor strengthening. Expected response: improved single leg stance and gait symmetry.
- **Hip flexion contracture:** Inability to achieve full hip extension limiting gait. Tested with stretching, prone lying, and manual therapy. Expected response: improved hip extension and step length.

## Rehabilitation Phases

**Phase 1 (0–2 weeks):** Precaution education, gait with walker, basic ROM and strengthening within precautions, edema management.

**Phase 2 (2–6 weeks):** Progressive strengthening, gait progression (walker → cane), stair training, increasing ROM.

**Phase 3 (6–12 weeks):** Precautions lifted (per surgeon), progressive functional training, balance, return to community activities.

**Phase 4 (3–6 months):** Full strengthening, return to recreational activities, sport-specific training if applicable. DISCHARGE when functional goals met.

## Non-Response
If gait and functional independence do not improve within expected timeframes, communicate with surgeon. Persistent limp may indicate leg length discrepancy, component malposition, or abductor insufficiency. Persistent pain may indicate loosening or infection requiring investigation.
