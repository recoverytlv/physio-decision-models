# Rotator Cuff Post-Surgical Rehabilitation

## Condition
Post-operative rehabilitation following rotator cuff repair (arthroscopic or open). Applies to partial and full-thickness tear repairs.

## Scope Check
Post-surgical rotator cuff rehabilitation is within physiotherapy scope when cleared by the surgeon. Suspected re-tear, infection, or stiffness not responding to treatment triggers communication with surgeon or REFER.

## Red Flags (→ REFER)
- Sudden loss of strength after initial recovery (suspected re-tear)
- Wound infection signs (redness, warmth, drainage, fever)
- Significant increase in pain not explained by rehabilitation progression
- Inability to achieve passive ROM milestones despite appropriate treatment (possible adhesive capsulitis requiring surgical consultation)

## Clinical Hypothesis Examples
- **Post-surgical stiffness:** Capsular adhesion limiting passive ROM after repair. Tested with progressive passive and active-assisted ROM within surgical protocol limits. Expected response: measurable ROM gains at each evaluation.
- **Rotator cuff activation deficit:** Difficulty activating repaired cuff muscles. Tested with isometric activation, biofeedback, and progressive resistance. Expected response: improved active ROM and cuff strength within each phase.
- **Scapular dyskinesis:** Altered scapular mechanics compensating for cuff weakness. Tested with scapular stabilization exercises. Expected response: improved scapular positioning and reduced compensatory patterns.

## Rehabilitation Phases and Decision Points
Each phase functions as a TRIAL cycle:

**Phase 1 (0–6 weeks):** Protected passive ROM, sling management. CONTINUE if passive ROM improves per protocol.

**Phase 2 (6–12 weeks):** Active-assisted to active ROM, begin isometric strengthening. CONTINUE if active ROM improves.

**Phase 3 (3–6 months):** Progressive strengthening, functional loading. CONTINUE if strength improves.

**Phase 4 (6–12 months):** Return to full function, sport-specific loading if applicable. DISCHARGE when functional goals met.

## Non-Response
If ROM or strength milestones are not met within expected timeframes, communicate with surgeon. Persistent stiffness may require manipulation under anesthesia or arthroscopic capsular release. Persistent weakness may indicate re-tear requiring imaging.
