# Shoulder Instability

## Condition
Glenohumeral joint instability ranging from traumatic dislocation (anterior, posterior, or multidirectional) to atraumatic instability (generalized hypermobility, repetitive microtrauma). Includes first-time dislocations, recurrent instability, subluxation, and post-surgical stabilization rehabilitation.

## Scope Check
Shoulder instability rehabilitation is within physiotherapy scope. Acute dislocation requiring reduction, suspected fracture-dislocation, recurrent instability not responding to rehabilitation, and neurovascular compromise require REFER.

## Red Flags (→ REFER)
- Acute dislocation that has not been reduced (emergency — immediate referral for reduction)
- Suspected fracture with dislocation (Hill-Sachs, Bankart, greater tuberosity fracture)
- Neurovascular deficit after dislocation (axillary nerve palsy — deltoid weakness, regimental badge numbness)
- Recurrent dislocations (>2 episodes) despite comprehensive rehabilitation (surgical stabilization consultation)
- First-time dislocation in a young athlete (<25 years) with high recurrence risk (early surgical consultation may be appropriate)

## Classification

| Type | Mechanism | Characteristics | Management |
|---|---|---|---|
| **TUBS** | Traumatic, Unidirectional, Bankart lesion, Surgery often needed | Single direction instability, clear trauma | Rehab → if recurrent, surgery |
| **AMBRI** | Atraumatic, Multidirectional, Bilateral, Rehabilitation, Inferior capsule shift if needed | Generalized laxity, no single trauma | Rehabilitation is primary treatment |

## Clinical Hypothesis Examples
- **Post-traumatic anterior instability (conservative):** First-time anterior dislocation managed conservatively. Tested with progressive rotator cuff and scapular stabilization strengthening. Expected response: improved shoulder confidence, reduced apprehension test provocation, and return to function within 8–12 weeks.
- **Multidirectional instability:** Generalized ligamentous laxity with shoulder symptoms. Tested with intensive rotator cuff and scapular stabilizer strengthening (long-term program). Expected response: improved dynamic stability, reduced symptoms with overhead activities. Note: this condition requires longer rehabilitation (3–6 months).
- **Post-surgical stabilization (Bankart repair/Latarjet):** After surgical stabilization. Tested with progressive ROM, then strengthening, then sport-specific loading per surgical protocol. Expected response: ROM and strength milestones met within each phase.
- **Scapular dyskinesis contributing to instability:** Poor scapular control increasing demands on glenohumeral stabilizers. Tested with scapular stabilization program. Expected response: improved scapular positioning and reduced shoulder symptoms.

## Rehabilitation Phases (Conservative and Post-Surgical)

**Phase 1 (0–6 weeks post-injury/surgery):** Protected ROM, sling management (if post-surgical), isometric rotator cuff activation, scapular setting exercises. No end-range external rotation (anterior instability).

**Phase 2 (6–12 weeks):** Progressive active ROM, begin isotonic strengthening of rotator cuff and scapular stabilizers. Proprioceptive training. Progress external rotation within tolerance.

**Phase 3 (3–6 months):** Full ROM restoration, progressive resistance training, functional loading, perturbation training. Begin sport-specific preparation.

**Phase 4 (6–9 months):** Return to sport testing, plyometric loading, contact preparation if applicable. DISCHARGE when functional goals met and apprehension negative.

## Trial Window
5 sessions for initial conservative management. Objective criteria:
- Improved shoulder confidence (reduced apprehension)
- Improved rotator cuff strength (measured, compared bilaterally)
- Improved scapular control (observation, endurance tests)
- Improved functional capacity (overhead activities, sport-specific)

## Non-Response
If conservative management fails (recurrent instability episodes, persistent apprehension limiting function), refer for surgical consultation. Young athletes (<25) with traumatic anterior dislocation have high recurrence rates (>50%) — early surgical discussion may be appropriate even after first dislocation.
