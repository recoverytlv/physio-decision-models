# Neck Pain

## Condition
Neck pain including mechanical cervical pain, cervical disc herniation, whiplash-associated disorders, and cervicogenic headache.

## Scope Check
Neck pain is within physiotherapy scope when musculoskeletal in origin. Upper motor neuron signs, vertebrobasilar insufficiency symptoms, or progressive myelopathy trigger REFER.

## Red Flags (→ REFER)
- Upper limb progressive weakness or bilateral symptoms (myelopathy)
- Drop attacks, diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia, dizziness with cervical movement (vertebrobasilar insufficiency)
- Trauma with suspected cervical fracture or instability
- Signs of spinal cord compression (gait disturbance, hyperreflexia, clonus)
- Fever with neck stiffness (meningitis)

## Clinical Hypothesis Examples
- **Cervical segmental dysfunction:** Restricted intersegmental mobility. Tested with manual therapy to target level. Expected response: improved cervical ROM and reduced local pain within 2–3 sessions.
- **Cervical radiculopathy:** Nerve root involvement with dermatomal arm pain. Tested with cervical traction or directional preference. Expected response: centralization of arm symptoms.
- **Whiplash-associated disorder:** Post-traumatic cervical sensitivity and motor control deficit. Tested with graded motor control and cervical stabilization. Expected response: improved deep neck flexor endurance and reduced movement fear.
- **Cervicogenic headache:** Headache driven by upper cervical dysfunction. Tested with C1-C2 mobilization and suboccipital techniques. Expected response: reduced headache frequency and intensity.

## Trial Window
3–5 sessions. Objective criteria:
- ≥10% improvement in cervical ROM (rotation, flexion, extension)
- Reduced headache frequency (for cervicogenic headache)
- Centralization of arm symptoms (for radiculopathy)
- Improved deep neck flexor endurance or motor control tests

## Non-Response
If cervical ROM does not improve or neurological symptoms progress, refer for specialist assessment. Persistent radiculopathy without centralization may require imaging.
