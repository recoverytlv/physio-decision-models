# Knee Osteoarthritis

## Definition
Degenerative joint disease of the knee with cartilage loss, subchondral bone changes, and synovial inflammation. Strong evidence supports physiotherapy (exercise therapy) as first-line treatment before surgical consideration.

## Why This Matters
International guidelines (OARSI, NICE, EULAR) consistently recommend exercise therapy and education as first-line treatment. Yet many patients are referred directly to surgery without adequate conservative management. This guide ensures structured conservative care with clear criteria for surgical referral.

## Red Flags → REFER
- Locked knee (mechanical block to extension) — possible loose body
- Acute hot, swollen joint with fever — septic arthritis (emergency)
- Rapid joint destruction on imaging
- Crystal arthropathy suspected (acute gout/pseudogout) — rheumatology
- Significant valgus/varus instability with functional limitation despite optimal conservative care → orthopedic opinion

## Classification by Severity

| Grade | Clinical Presentation | Physiotherapy Approach |
|-------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Mild | Occasional pain, no/minimal effusion, full ROM | Education, exercise program, activity modification |
| Moderate | Regular pain with activity, mild effusion, some ROM loss, quad weakness | Structured strengthening, manual therapy, weight management support |
| Severe | Pain at rest, significant ROM loss, functional limitation, failed conservative care | Optimize function, pre-surgical prehab if surgery planned |

## Clinical Hypothesis Framework

### Load Management Hypothesis
- **Hypothesis:** Knee pain is driven by load exceeding tissue capacity
- **Test:** Graded exercise program (quad strengthening, functional training), activity modification
- **Expected response:** Pain reduction with activity, improved walk distance/stair ability within 6-8 sessions
- **If no response:** Consider biomechanical factors, central sensitization, or surgical candidacy

### Quadriceps Weakness Hypothesis
- **Hypothesis:** Arthrogenic muscle inhibition and disuse weakness drive functional limitation
- **Test:** Progressive quadriceps strengthening (isometric → isotonic → functional)
- **Expected response:** Measurable strength gains, improved functional tests (sit-to-stand, stairs, TUG)
- **If no response:** Consider other limiting factors (pain catastrophizing, comorbidities)

### Biomechanical Hypothesis
- **Hypothesis:** Altered mechanics (varus thrust, hip weakness, foot pronation) increase medial compartment load
- **Test:** Hip strengthening, gait retraining, orthotic trial if indicated
- **Expected response:** Reduced pain with walking, improved gait quality

## Evidence-Based Exercise Prescription

| Exercise Type | Evidence Level | Dosing |
|--------------|---------------|--------|
| Quadriceps strengthening | Strong | 3x/week, progressive loading |
| Hip abductor strengthening | Strong | 3x/week |
| Aerobic exercise (walking, cycling, swimming) | Strong | 150 min/week |
| Aquatic therapy | Moderate | 2-3x/week if land-based too painful |
| Balance/proprioception | Moderate | Integrated into program |
| Flexibility (hamstrings, calves, quads) | Moderate | Daily |

## What NOT to Do
- Do not advise "rest and avoid activity" — deconditioning worsens outcomes
- Do not rely on passive treatments alone (ultrasound, TENS) — active exercise is the intervention
- Do not tell patient "bone on bone" means nothing can help — exercise consistently reduces pain even with severe radiographic changes
- Do not use imaging findings alone to determine treatment — clinical presentation matters more

## Trial Protocol (TRIAL)
| Session | Focus |
|---------|-------|
| 1 | Assessment, baseline functional tests (TUG, 30s sit-to-stand, 6MWT), education |
| 2-3 | Establish exercise program, address barriers (fear, pain beliefs) |
| 4-5 | Re-evaluate functional tests vs baseline |
| 6-8 | Extension if improving: progress loading, add functional tasks |

## Decision Criteria

### CONTINUE if:
- Functional test improvement (TUG, sit-to-stand, walk distance)
- Pain reduction with activity
- Strength gains documented
- Patient engagement with home exercise

### DISCHARGE if:
- Functional goals met
- Independent exercise program established
- Self-management strategies in place

### REFER if:
- Red flags (septic joint, locked knee)
- Failed adequate conservative trial (≥8-12 weeks structured exercise)
- Severe functional limitation despite optimal conservative care → orthopedic opinion
- Significant psychological barriers → psychology

## Pre-Surgical Prehabilitation
If surgery is planned (TKA), prehab improves post-surgical outcomes:
- 4-6 weeks pre-surgery: quad strengthening, ROM optimization, aerobic fitness
- Education about post-surgical expectations
