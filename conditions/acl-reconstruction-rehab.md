# ACL Reconstruction Rehabilitation

## Condition
Post-operative rehabilitation following anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) reconstruction. Applies to all graft types (hamstring, patellar tendon, quadriceps tendon, allograft).

## Scope Check
Post-ACL reconstruction rehabilitation is within physiotherapy scope. Suspected graft failure, infection, or DVT trigger REFER.

## Red Flags (→ REFER)
- Sudden giving way or instability suggesting graft failure
- Increasing effusion with warmth and redness (infection)
- Calf pain with swelling (DVT)
- Inability to achieve expected milestones despite appropriate rehabilitation (re-evaluate with surgeon)
- Cyclops lesion signs (mechanical block to terminal extension)

## Clinical Hypothesis Examples
- **Quadriceps inhibition:** Arthrogenic muscle inhibition limiting quad activation. Tested with neuromuscular electrical stimulation and targeted quadriceps exercises. Expected response: improved active extension and quad strength within each evaluation cycle.
- **Extension deficit:** Loss of terminal knee extension. Tested with prone hangs and extension mobilization. Expected response: restoration of symmetrical extension.
- **Flexion limitation:** Restricted knee flexion from capsular adhesion. Tested with progressive flexion mobilization. Expected response: measurable flexion gains at each evaluation point.
- **Functional loading progression:** Progressive loading through phases (isometric → isotonic → plyometric → sport-specific). Expected response: improved single leg squat, hop test performance, and loading tolerance at each phase.

## Rehabilitation Phases and Decision Points
Each phase functions as a TRIAL cycle within the clinical decision system:

**Phase 1 (0–6 weeks):** Restore extension, reduce effusion, activate quadriceps. CONTINUE if extension improves and quad activation returns.

**Phase 2 (6–12 weeks):** Restore flexion, improve strength, begin closed chain loading. CONTINUE if ROM normalizes and strength improves.

**Phase 3 (3–6 months):** Progressive strengthening, single leg loading. CONTINUE if strength approaches symmetry.

**Phase 4 (6–9 months):** Running, agility, plyometrics. CONTINUE if functional tests improve.

**Phase 5 (9–12 months):** Return-to-sport testing. DISCHARGE when criteria met, or CONTINUE if objective improvement ongoing.

## Return-to-Sport Criteria
- Knee extension and flexion ROM symmetrical to uninjured side
- Quadriceps strength ≥90% of uninjured side (isokinetic or dynamometry)
- Hop test battery ≥90% limb symmetry index
- Single leg squat with good biomechanics
- Sport-specific drills completed without apprehension or compensatory patterns

## Non-Response
If expected milestones are not met within each phase, reassess. Persistent extension deficit after Phase 1 may require surgical consultation (cyclops lesion). Persistent strength deficit may require hypothesis revision (graft site pain, kinesiophobia, neural inhibition).
