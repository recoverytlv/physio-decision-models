# Total Knee Replacement Rehabilitation

## Condition
Post-operative rehabilitation following total knee arthroplasty (TKA). Includes primary and revision knee replacement procedures.

## Scope Check
Post-surgical knee replacement rehabilitation is within physiotherapy scope when cleared by the surgeon. Signs of surgical complication (infection, DVT, component failure) require immediate REFER.

## Red Flags (→ REFER)
- Calf pain, swelling, and redness (suspected deep vein thrombosis — DVT)
- Sudden shortness of breath with chest pain (suspected pulmonary embolism — emergency)
- Wound infection signs (increasing redness, warmth, drainage, fever)
- Sudden loss of ROM after initial progress (suspected arthrofibrosis or component malposition)
- Persistent instability or giving way (component loosening or ligament insufficiency)
- New-onset numbness or foot drop (peroneal nerve palsy)

## Clinical Hypothesis Examples
- **Post-surgical stiffness:** Swelling and capsular tightness limiting flexion and extension. Tested with progressive ROM exercises, patellar mobilization, and edema management. Expected response: measurable ROM gains at each visit (target: 0° extension, 90° flexion by 6 weeks, 110-120° by 12 weeks).
- **Quadriceps activation deficit:** Difficulty activating the quadriceps after surgery (arthrogenic muscle inhibition). Tested with electrical stimulation, biofeedback, isometric quad sets, and straight leg raise. Expected response: improved active extension lag and quad contraction quality.
- **Gait dysfunction:** Altered gait pattern post-surgery (antalgic gait, Trendelenburg, knee hyperextension). Tested with gait training, progressive weight bearing, and strengthening. Expected response: improved gait symmetry and reduced assistive device dependence.

## Rehabilitation Phases and Decision Points

**Phase 1 (0–2 weeks):** Wound care, edema management, ROM (target 0-90°), quad activation, gait with walker.
- CONTINUE if ROM progresses and wound is healing

**Phase 2 (2–6 weeks):** Progressive ROM (target 0-110°), strengthening, gait progression (walker → cane).
- CONTINUE if ROM and strength improve per targets

**Phase 3 (6–12 weeks):** Full ROM restoration, progressive strengthening, functional training (stairs, squatting).
- CONTINUE if functional milestones are met

**Phase 4 (3–6 months):** Return to full function, sport/recreational activities as appropriate.
- DISCHARGE when functional goals met

## Non-Response
If ROM milestones are not met (especially <90° flexion at 6 weeks or persistent extension lag), communicate with surgeon. Early arthrofibrosis may require manipulation under anesthesia. Persistent quad inhibition not responding to standard approaches may need further investigation.
