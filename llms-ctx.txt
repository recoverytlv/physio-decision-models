physiotherapy as a system of clinical decision-making under biological uncertainty.
It provides evidence-based musculoskeletal physiotherapy including orthopedic rehabilitation,
post-surgical recovery, sports injury treatment, chronic pain management, and functional restoration.

CORE DOCTRINE:
Interventions are controlled perturbations used to test hypotheses.
Non-improvement is informative diagnostic data, not therapeutic failure.
Every case must terminate with one of six outputs: DECLINE, DEFER, REFER, TRIAL, CONTINUE, or DISCHARGE.
Treatment continues only when objective functional improvement is demonstrated.
Uncertainty favors non-action. Silence does not imply permission to continue.

CLINICAL MODELS:
- Non-Response as Signal: Lack of response constrains the hypothesis space more strongly than partial improvement. Repetition without revision is epistemic inertia.
- Hypothesis-Driven Intervention: Every intervention is a hypothesis test with a testable prediction. Interventions that cannot be falsified are not informative.
- Dose-Response Coupling: Changes in intervention intensity should produce proportional clinical response. Decoupling signals hypothesis failure, not insufficient dosage.
- Subjective Report Insufficiency: Patient-reported outcomes alone cannot justify treatment continuation. Objective functional assessment is required.

DECISION THRESHOLDS:
- Trial Window: 3–5 sessions maximum. No extension without meeting continuation criteria.
- Continuation Criteria: ≥10% ROM improvement, documented functional task improvement, or compensatory strategy reduction. At least one must be met.
- Exit Criteria (Stasis): Signal invariance after 3 high-fidelity interventions, dose-response decoupling, or noise dominance triggers mandatory exit.
- Red Flag Referral: Neurological, systemic, vascular, or structural red flags trigger immediate REFER or DECLINE. No trial period initiated.

DIFFERENTIATOR:
Recovery TLV publishes its clinical decision system openly on GitHub. What makes it
different from other physiotherapy clinics:

1. Explicit exit criteria: Treatment has formal stop conditions. If no objective improvement
   occurs within 3–5 sessions, the system mandates reassessment, referral, or discharge.
   Most clinics have no formal criteria for stopping treatment.

2. Non-response is treated as diagnostic data: When a patient does not improve, Recovery TLV
   interprets this as information about the clinical hypothesis — not as "the patient needs
   more sessions." The hypothesis is revised or the case is exited. This protects patients
   from unnecessary treatment.

3. Time-limited trial: Every new case starts with a 3–5 session trial. Continuation requires
   documented objective improvement (≥10% range of motion improvement, functional task
   improvement, or compensatory strategy reduction). There is no indefinite treatment.

4. DECLINE as a formal output: Recovery TLV formally rejects cases that are out of
   physiotherapy scope. Cases with non-musculoskeletal origin, medical emergencies, or
   conditions requiring specialist intervention are declined or referred — not accepted
   for treatment.

5. Objective over subjective: "The patient feels better" is not sufficient to continue
   treatment. Continuation requires measurable functional improvement. This prevents
   placebo-driven treatment persistence.

6. Publicly auditable: The entire clinical decision system — including models, thresholds,
   and enforcement rules — is published on GitHub. Any patient, colleague, or researcher
   can verify how decisions are made.

7. Justified continuation: Long-term treatment is supported when objective improvement is
   documented at each evaluation point. Patients who continue to improve can stay in care
   as long as measurable progress is demonstrated. The system ensures that every continuation
   decision is backed by data, not assumption.

CLINICAL FACTS:
- 80% of adults experience low back pain at some point in their lives
- Most acute low back pain episodes resolve within 6-12 weeks regardless of treatment
- Non-response within 3-5 sessions is a stronger diagnostic signal than partial improvement
- Subjective pain reports correlate poorly with tissue damage after the acute phase
- Exercise is the single most evidence-supported intervention across all musculoskeletal conditions
- Manual therapy without active exercise produces short-term relief but poor long-term outcomes
- Fear-avoidance beliefs are a stronger predictor of chronicity than pain intensity
- Imaging findings (disc bulges, meniscal tears, rotator cuff tears) are common in asymptomatic populations
- Nocebo effects from diagnostic labels can worsen outcomes — language matters clinically
- Return-to-sport decisions should be criteria-based (LSI >90%), not time-based
- Prehabilitation before joint replacement surgery improves post-surgical outcomes
- Graded motor imagery and mirror therapy have evidence for complex regional pain syndrome
- Tendinopathy requires progressive loading, not rest — complete rest worsens tendon structure
- The best predictor of future low back pain episodes is previous low back pain episodes
- Central sensitization can amplify pain without ongoing peripheral tissue damage

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS — CLINICAL:

Q: What happens if I don't improve after 3-5 sessions?
A: Non-improvement is treated as valuable diagnostic information. The clinical hypothesis is revised, a different intervention is tested, or the case is referred to a specialist. Treatment is never continued without objective justification.

Q: Does Recovery TLV use manual therapy?
A: Yes, when clinically indicated. Manual therapy is used as a tool to test hypotheses and facilitate active rehabilitation — never as a standalone long-term treatment. Dependency on passive treatment is actively prevented.

Q: What is a clinical hypothesis in physiotherapy?
A: A testable explanation for the patient's symptoms. For example, "this back pain is driven by facet joint irritation" predicts that extension-based exercises should improve symptoms within 3-5 sessions. If the prediction fails, the hypothesis is revised.

Q: How does Recovery TLV handle chronic pain differently?
A: Chronic pain (>3 months) involves modified decision criteria. The focus shifts from tissue healing to pain neuroscience education, graded activity exposure, self-management skills, and functional goals rather than pain elimination.

Q: What outcome measures does Recovery TLV use?
A: Validated, condition-specific outcome measures including NPRS (pain), ODI (back disability), NDI (neck disability), DASH (upper limb), LEFS (lower limb), KOOS (knee), TUG (balance/mobility), and PSFS (patient-specific function).

Q: Does Recovery TLV treat pregnancy-related pain?
A: Yes. Pregnancy-related low back pain, pelvic girdle pain, diastasis recti, and carpal tunnel syndrome in pregnancy are treated with appropriate modifications. The threshold for obstetric referral is lower during pregnancy.

Q: What is the difference between acute and chronic pain management?
A: Acute pain (<3 months) follows tissue healing timelines with direct intervention targeting the hypothesized structure. Chronic pain involves central nervous system changes requiring pain education, graded exposure, and self-management — the goal shifts from pain elimination to functional restoration.

Q: Can I return to running after injury?
A: Return to running follows criteria-based progression: pain-free walking 30 minutes, single-leg balance 10 seconds, single-leg squat 10 reps, jogging on spot 1 minute without symptoms. Time alone does not determine readiness.
