# Pregnancy-Related Musculoskeletal Conditions

## Scope

Musculoskeletal conditions during pregnancy and postpartum period that fall within physiotherapy scope. Physiotherapy for pregnancy focuses on pain management, function maintenance, birth preparation, and postpartum recovery.

## Scope Boundaries
- **IN scope:** Low back pain in pregnancy, pelvic girdle pain, diastasis recti, postpartum recovery, carpal tunnel in pregnancy, thoracic pain, exercise prescription
- **OUT of scope:** Obstetric complications (preeclampsia, placenta previa, preterm labor), high-risk pregnancy management, pelvic organ prolapse (REFER to pelvic floor specialist)

## Red Flags — REFER Immediately

| Red Flag | Concern |
|----------|---------|
| Vaginal bleeding | Obstetric emergency |
| Regular contractions before 37 weeks | Preterm labor |
| Severe headache + visual changes + swelling | Preeclampsia |
| Sudden severe abdominal pain | Placental abruption or other emergency |
| Decreased fetal movement | Fetal distress |
| Fever >38°C | Infection |
| Calf pain + swelling (pregnancy increases DVT risk 5x) | DVT → REFER urgently |
| Leaking amniotic fluid | Premature rupture of membranes |

**Rule:** When in doubt about ANY symptom during pregnancy → REFER to obstetrician. The threshold for referral is LOWER during pregnancy.

## Common Pregnancy-Related Conditions

### 1. Pregnancy-Related Low Back Pain (PLBP)
**Prevalence:** 50-70% of pregnancies

**Mechanism:** Anterior shift of center of gravity, increased lumbar lordosis, ligament laxity from relaxin, weakened abdominal wall support.

**Hypothesis:** Lumbar overload due to biomechanical changes and reduced core stability.

**Management:**
- Pelvic floor and deep core activation (transversus abdominis)
- Posterior pelvic tilt exercises
- Cat-cow stretches
- Aquatic exercise (buoyancy reduces load)
- Pelvic support belt if needed
- Sleeping position: side-lying with pillow between knees

**What NOT to do:**
- No prone lying (obvious, but worth stating)
- No high-force lumbar manipulation
- No exercises in supine position after 16-20 weeks (supine hypotension syndrome)
- No heavy resistance training without supervision

### 2. Pelvic Girdle Pain (PGP)
**Prevalence:** 20-30% of pregnancies

**Presentation:** Pain at sacroiliac joints (SIJ) and/or pubic symphysis. Worse with: walking, stairs, turning in bed, single-leg stance.

**Hypothesis:** SIJ instability due to hormonal ligament laxity + altered load distribution.

**Management:**
- Pelvic stability exercises (avoid wide-stance exercises)
- SIJ belt for compression and support
- Activity modification: keep knees together when turning in bed, avoid single-leg stance
- Avoid asymmetric loading (carrying child on one hip)
- Gentle hip adductor strengthening (if symphysis pain)
- Ice for acute flares

**Decision criteria:**
- CONTINUE if function maintained/improved
- REFER if severe pain limiting mobility, suspected symphysis diastasis (gap >10mm)

### 3. Diastasis Recti Abdominis (DRA)
**Definition:** Separation of rectus abdominis muscles at the linea alba. Common in pregnancy and postpartum.

**Assessment:** Palpate gap above and below umbilicus. >2 finger widths is considered clinically significant.

**Timing:**
- During pregnancy: gentle core exercises, avoid aggravating movements
- Postpartum (6 weeks+): progressive core rehabilitation

**Management:**
- Deep core activation (transversus abdominis + pelvic floor co-contraction)
- Avoid: sit-ups, crunches, front planks, heavy lifting until gap reduces
- Progressive loading: supine → side-lying → sitting → standing → functional
- Most DRA improves significantly in first 8 weeks postpartum

**REFER if:** DRA >3 finger widths at 8 weeks postpartum with no improvement, or umbilical hernia present.

### 4. Carpal Tunnel Syndrome in Pregnancy
**Prevalence:** Up to 60% experience symptoms, usually in 3rd trimester.

**Mechanism:** Fluid retention compresses median nerve in carpal tunnel.

**Management:**
- Night wrist splinting in neutral position
- Nerve gliding exercises
- Elevation and gentle hand exercises
- Usually resolves postpartum (within weeks-months)

**REFER if:** Severe weakness, persistent thenar wasting, no resolution by 3 months postpartum.

### 5. Thoracic Pain in Pregnancy
**Mechanism:** Breast enlargement, increased thoracic kyphosis, rib cage expansion.

**Management:**
- Thoracic extension exercises (seated/standing, not prone)
- Scapular retraction strengthening
- Supportive bra
- Breathing exercises (rib cage expansion with diaphragm)

## Exercise During Pregnancy — Guidelines

### General Recommendations (ACOG-based)
- 150 minutes moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week
- Strength training 2x/week with appropriate modifications
- Pelvic floor exercises daily
- Avoid contact sports, fall-risk activities, hot environments

### Safe Exercises

| Trimester | Recommended | Modifications |
|-----------|-------------|--------------|
| 1st (0-12 weeks) | Continue pre-pregnancy routine if uncomplicated | Reduce intensity if nausea/fatigue |
| 2nd (13-27 weeks) | Walking, swimming, stationary cycling, prenatal yoga, modified strength training | Avoid supine exercise after 16-20 weeks, widen stance for balance |
| 3rd (28-40 weeks) | Walking, swimming, pelvic floor, gentle stretching | Reduce intensity further, focus on comfort and function |

### Exercise Contraindications During Pregnancy (Absolute)
- Hemodynamically significant heart disease
- Restrictive lung disease
- Incompetent cervix
- Persistent bleeding in 2nd/3rd trimester
- Placenta previa after 26 weeks
- Premature labor in current pregnancy
- Ruptured membranes
- Preeclampsia/pregnancy-induced hypertension

### Warning Signs to STOP Exercise Immediately
- Vaginal bleeding
- Regular painful contractions
- Amniotic fluid leakage
- Dyspnea before exertion
- Dizziness or syncope
- Chest pain
- Calf pain or swelling
- Headache

## Postpartum Rehabilitation Timeline

| Phase | Timeframe | Focus |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| Early postpartum | 0-6 weeks | Gentle pelvic floor activation, walking, breathing. Respect healing (vaginal or C-section) |
| Recovery phase | 6-12 weeks | Progressive core rehab, DRA assessment, gradual return to exercise |
| Strengthening | 3-6 months | Progressive loading, address any ongoing issues (PGP, DRA, incontinence) |
| Return to sport | 6-12+ months | High-impact activities, running (see return-to-running criteria below) |

### Return to Running Postpartum (Minimum Criteria)
Based on Goom et al. (2019) guidelines:
- [ ] At least 3 months postpartum
- [ ] No pelvic floor dysfunction (incontinence, heaviness)
- [ ] Can walk 30 minutes without symptoms
- [ ] Single leg balance 10 seconds
- [ ] Single leg squat 10 reps each side
- [ ] Jog on spot 1 minute without symptoms
- [ ] Single leg hop 10 reps without symptoms
- [ ] DRA managed (not necessarily closed, but functional)

## Decision Criteria

### TRIAL
- Standard 3-5 sessions for specific conditions
- Extended assessment for complex postpartum cases

### CONTINUE if:
- Pain reduction allowing function
- Progressive core rehabilitation showing improvement
- DRA gap reducing
- Patient able to participate in daily activities

### DISCHARGE if:
- Functional goals met
- Independent exercise program
- Self-management strategies in place

### REFER if:
- Any obstetric red flag (see above)
- Pelvic organ prolapse symptoms → pelvic floor specialist
- Severe DRA not responding to conservative treatment → specialist
- Persistent incontinence >3 months postpartum → pelvic floor specialist
- Mental health concerns (postpartum depression) → psychology/psychiatry
