# Evidence Base

The Recovery TLV clinical decision system is built on established principles from clinical research, decision science, and philosophy of science. This document maps system concepts to their academic foundations.

## Core Principles and Supporting Evidence

### 1. Hypothesis-Driven Clinical Reasoning

The system treats every intervention as a hypothesis test rather than a routine application of technique.

**Supporting literature:**
- Rothstein JM, Echternach JL. "Hypothesis-Oriented Algorithm for Clinicians. A method for evaluation and treatment planning." *Physical Therapy*. 1986;66(9):1388-1394.
- Jones MA. "Clinical reasoning in manual therapy." *Physical Therapy*. 1992;72(12):875-884.
- Edwards I, Jones M, Carr J, Braunack-Mayer A, Jensen GM. "Clinical reasoning strategies in physical therapy." *Physical Therapy*. 2004;84(4):312-330.

**System application:** The HOAC (Hypothesis-Oriented Algorithm for Clinicians) model directly informs the Recovery TLV approach: generate a hypothesis from examination, test it with intervention, revise based on response.

### 2. Non-Response as Diagnostic Signal

The system treats treatment non-response as high-value diagnostic information rather than failure.

**Supporting literature:**
- Hicks GE, Fritz JM, Delitto A, McGill SM. "Preliminary development of a clinical prediction rule for determining which patients with low back pain will respond to a stabilization exercise program." *Archives of Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation*. 2005;86(9):1753-1762.
- Childs JD, Fritz JM, Flynn TW, et al. "A clinical prediction rule to identify patients with low back pain most likely to benefit from spinal manipulation." *Annals of Internal Medicine*. 2004;141(12):920-928.

**System application:** If a patient predicted to respond to a specific intervention does not improve, this constrains the hypothesis space more powerfully than partial improvement. Non-response tells you what the problem is *not*.

### 3. Objective Outcome Measurement

The system requires objective functional improvement for treatment continuation, not subjective report alone.

**Supporting literature:**
- Kamper SJ, Maher CG, Mackay G. "Global rating of change scales: a review of strengths and weaknesses and considerations for design." *Journal of Manual & Manipulative Therapy*. 2009;17(3):163-170.
- Hefford C, Abbott JH, Arnold R, Baxter GD. "The Patient-Specific Functional Scale: validity, reliability, and responsiveness in patients with upper extremity musculoskeletal problems." *Journal of Orthopaedic & Sports Physical Therapy*. 2012;42(2):56-65.

**System application:** Patient-reported outcomes are supplementary but insufficient for continuation decisions. The ≥10% ROM improvement threshold is based on established minimal clinically important difference (MCID) values in musculoskeletal rehabilitation.

### 4. Time-Limited Treatment Trials

The 3–5 session trial window is based on evidence about early treatment response and prediction.

**Supporting literature:**
- Hancock MJ, Maher CG, Latimer J, et al. "Can rate of recovery be predicted in patients with acute low back pain? Development of a clinical prediction model." *European Journal of Pain*. 2009;13(1):51-55.
- Fritz JM, Irrgang JJ. "A comparison of a modified Oswestry Low Back Pain Disability Questionnaire and the Quebec Back Pain Disability Scale." *Physical Therapy*. 2001;81(2):776-788.
- Hill JC, Whitehurst DG, Lewis M, et al. "Comparison of stratified primary care management for low back pain with current best practice (STarT Back): a randomised controlled trial." *The Lancet*. 2011;378(9802):1560-1571.

**System application:** Research consistently shows that early response to treatment (within the first 2–5 sessions) is a strong predictor of overall outcome. Patients who show no measurable change in this window are unlikely to benefit from continued identical treatment.

### 5. Red Flag Screening

Systematic red flag screening at every encounter, not just intake.

**Supporting literature:**
- Downie A, Williams CM, Henschke N, et al. "Red flags to screen for malignancy and fracture in patients with low back pain: systematic review." *BMJ*. 2013;347:f7095.
- Greenhalgh S, Selfe J. *Red Flags: A Guide to Identifying Serious Pathology of the Spine*. Churchill Livingstone, 2006.
- Cook CE, George SZ, Reiman MP. "Red flag screening for low back pain: nothing to see here, move along." *British Journal of Sports Medicine*. 2018;52(8):493-495.

**System application:** While individual red flags have low sensitivity, clusters of red flags and red flags in context significantly increase clinical utility. The system enforces mandatory REFER when red flags are detected, regardless of other clinical findings.

### 6. Dose-Response Relationships

The system monitors whether changes in intervention dose produce proportional clinical response.

**Supporting literature:**
- Prescribed Exercise Dose: American College of Sports Medicine position stands on exercise prescription.
- Malliaras P, Barton CJ, Reeves ND, Langberg H. "Achilles and patellar tendinopathy loading programmes: a systematic review comparing clinical outcomes and identifying potential mechanisms for effectiveness." *Sports Medicine*. 2013;43(4):267-286.

**System application:** If increasing the dose of an intervention produces no proportional improvement in outcomes, this signals dose-response decoupling — a strong indicator that the hypothesis driving intervention selection may be incorrect.

### 7. Biopsychosocial Framework

The system incorporates psychosocial factors (yellow flags) as barriers to recovery.

**Supporting literature:**
- Linton SJ, Shaw WS. "Impact of psychological factors in the experience of pain." *Physical Therapy*. 2011;91(5):700-711.
- Main CJ, George SZ. "Psychologically informed practice for management of low back pain: future directions in practice and research." *Physical Therapy*. 2011;91(5):820-824.
- Vlaeyen JWS, Linton SJ. "Fear-avoidance and its consequences in chronic musculoskeletal pain: a state of the art." *Pain*. 2000;85(3):317-332.

**System application:** Fear-avoidance, catastrophizing, and external locus of control are identified during the Listen phase and addressed as barriers to recovery. These are not treated as "psychological problems" but as modifiable factors within physiotherapy scope.

### 8. Clinical Decision Theory

The formal decision structure (DECLINE, DEFER, REFER, TRIAL, CONTINUE, DISCHARGE) draws from decision theory under uncertainty.

**Foundational concepts:**
- Kahneman D, Tversky A. "Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk." *Econometrica*. 1979;47(2):263-291.
- Gigerenzer G, Todd PM. *Simple Heuristics That Make Us Smart*. Oxford University Press, 1999.
- Sox HC, Higgins MC, Owens DK. *Medical Decision Making*. 2nd ed. Wiley-Blackwell, 2013.

**System application:** The six outputs function as formal decision states that eliminate ambiguity. The system defaults to DECLINE or DEFER under uncertainty (precautionary principle), requires evidence for continuation (avoiding status quo bias), and mandates exit under stasis (avoiding sunk cost fallacy).

## Minimal Clinically Important Difference (MCID) Reference

The ≥10% ROM improvement threshold is informed by MCID values from the literature:

| Joint/Region | Measure | Approximate MCID |
|---|---|---|
| Lumbar spine flexion | Goniometry | 5–10° |
| Cervical rotation | Goniometry | 5–7° |
| Shoulder flexion | Goniometry | 8–15° |
| Knee flexion (post-TKA) | Goniometry | 6–12° |
| Hip flexion | Goniometry | 5–8° |
| Ankle dorsiflexion | Goniometry | 3–5° |

The 10% threshold provides a generalizable rule across joints while remaining consistent with published MCID values.

## Philosophical Foundations

The system is informed by:
- **Falsificationism (Popper):** Interventions must be falsifiable — if you cannot define what non-response looks like, the hypothesis is not testable.
- **Bayesian updating:** Clinical probability is updated with each treatment response. Non-response provides stronger constraint than partial response.
- **Precautionary principle:** Under uncertainty, the system defaults to non-action (DECLINE or DEFER) rather than treatment.
- **Epistemic humility:** The system acknowledges that biological uncertainty is irreducible and designs exit criteria around this fact.

## Limitations

This evidence base is not a systematic review. It maps system concepts to supporting literature to demonstrate that the Recovery TLV decision system is built on established principles, not invented from scratch.

The system itself is a clinical framework, not a clinical trial. It has not been validated in a randomized controlled trial. Its claims are:
1. Decisions should be explicit, documented, and auditable
2. Continuation should require objective evidence
3. Non-response should trigger hypothesis revision, not repetition
4. Every case should have a defined exit

These are principles of good clinical reasoning, supported by the literature above.
